Faith, Power and Inequality

How religious institutions and oligarchic elites sustain extractive economic systems in the Philippines through theological legitimation, political capture, and the commodification of mass devotion.

6 core findings

1

The Philippines is the most Catholic and most religious country in Asia, yet it sustains one of the region's most entrenched oligarchic systems. 86% of Filipinos are Catholic. 88% say religion is "very important" (Pew 2025). Yet the top 40 families control 76% of publicly listed corporations and capture approximately 70% of GDP over multi-decade accumulation (IRC 2026). This is not a paradox. It is a designed system.

2

Catholic doctrine defines "social sin" as structures that cause systemic harm—yet the Philippine Church accepts funding from the architects of those structures. Catechism 1868 defines social sin as structures embedding injustice. The same oligarchs who extract rents, manipulate markets, and evade taxes donate to the Church (Zobel de Ayala: $15M church renovation; Villar: Manila Cathedral funding). These donations represent 0.003% of net worth—institutional legitimacy purchased at negligible cost.

3

Colonial theology installed a fatalistic worldview that re-codes exploitation as divine will. Spanish colonial rule imposed ritual without doctrinal depth. Indigenous fatalism (bahala na) merged with Catholic theology. This produces a cognitive framework where structural harm is experienced as God's plan, not human design. The result: passivity toward injustice is spiritualized as faith.

4

Non-Catholic institutions extract through different mechanisms but serve the same oligarchic order. Iglesia ni Cristo (INC) commodifies its 2–3 million-member voting bloc, trading endorsements to candidates in exchange for political access and tax exemptions. Apollo Quiboloy's Kingdom of Jesus Christ extracted tithes and labour from members while funnelling resources to allies of the Marcos and Duterte families.

5

The arithmetic of extraction clarifies the scale of harm. Power oligarchs extract $8B/year (21% of OFW remittances). Marcos family harm: $165–230B (3.7 years of OFW remittances). Villar's SEC fraud: $14–20B (9–12 years of grant ODA). Average OFW sends home $17,500/year; her family pays $400–800/year above ASEAN benchmark electricity rates to the same oligarchs.

6

The system works from above, below, and within simultaneously. From above: Catholic oligarchs donate to the Church at 0.003% of net worth. From below: INC extracts tithes and converts voting power into currency traded with oligarchs. From within: bahala na theology suppresses the cognitive dissonance that would otherwise drive collective action.

Section 01

The Paradox of Philippine Catholicism

The Philippines is the most Catholic country in Asia. Eighty-six per cent of its population identifies as Catholic. Across every socioeconomic decile, religious observance is not merely high but foundational to identity. Eighty-eight per cent of Filipinos say religion is "very important" in their lives — the highest in Asia and among the highest globally. In surveys of the importance of God, the Philippines ranks first or second in the world, sometimes ahead of Egypt.

Yet this intensely Catholic, deeply religious society sustains one of the most entrenched oligarchic systems in Southeast Asia. Forty families control 76 per cent of publicly listed corporations. Political dynasties span multiple generations, with family names cycling through the presidency, Senate, and House as if by hereditary right. Economic concentration has barely shifted since the 1990s. Inequality Gini coefficients remain high. The top one per cent of earners captures 17 per cent of national income; the bottom 50 per cent earns 14 per cent.

The question is not new. The structure has been analysed in econometrics, political science, and historical sociology. But what has received less forensic attention is how the religious infrastructure of the Philippines does not merely coexist with extreme inequality but actively sustains it — through theology, institutional practice, political alliance, and the commodification of mass devotion.

This is not an analysis of hypocrisy. It is not an argument that the wealthy are insincere in their faith or that the Church is consciously duplicitous. The correct investigative framework is structural, not moral. The question is: how does an intensely religious society produce and reproduce oligarchic capture across generations without triggering mass resistance rooted in the same theological tradition that condemns greed and commands justice?

The answer is that the system was designed for exactly this outcome.

Section 02

The Structural Theology of Exploitation

Philippine Catholicism is distinct from Latin American or European variants in one critical respect: it is a faith of ritual without doctrine. This is not accidental. It was installed.

Spanish colonial rule lasted 333 years. Conversion was imposed through friars, not missionaries trained in theology. The goal was not theological sophistication but behavioural compliance. The result is what Jesuit scholar Jaime Bulatao termed "split-level Christianity" in 1966 — a surface Catholicism layered over indigenous animist and fatalist worldviews that were never fully displaced.

The critical theological residue is bahala na — "leave it to God" or "it's up to fate." In indigenous Tagalog cosmology, bahala na functioned as adaptive fatalism in an environment of typhoons, volcanic eruptions, and subsistence agriculture. When merged with Catholic theology under colonial rule, it became a theological justification for accepting one's station in life as divinely ordained.

The theology does not say: "God commands you to resist injustice." It says: "God has a plan for you, and your suffering is part of it."

This produces a cognitive framework in which exploitation is not experienced as human agency to be resisted, but as divine will to be endured. Families borrow money to fund fiestas honouring patron saints — a practice that originated in Spanish colonial tax extraction but is now performed as voluntary devotion. The fiesta system is not a cultural quirk. It is a financial drain at retail scale, repeated across every barangay, that functions as a regressive tax on the poor.

The average fiesta expenditure for a low-income family is 15–25% of annual household income. This money goes to food, alcohol, decorations, entertainment — all of which must be procured, often on credit. The debt is repaid over months or years. Meanwhile, the same families pay electricity rates 30–50% above the ASEAN average to oligarch-controlled power monopolies. The theology that makes borrowing for a fiesta feel like a moral obligation is the same theology that makes accepting above-market electricity rates feel like an unchangeable fact of life.

The theology of social sin — ignored

Catholic doctrine does contain a robust framework for diagnosing structural injustice. The Catechism of the Catholic Church, paragraphs 1849 and 1866–1868, defines "social sin" as structures of sin that embed injustice across society. Vatican statements in 2008 clarified that social sin refers to institutions and systems — not merely to individual moral failures.

Paragraph 1868 states: "Sin is a personal act. Moreover, we have a responsibility for the sins committed by others when we cooperate in them... by not disclosing or not hindering them when we have an obligation to do so; by protecting evil-doers."

This is not obscure theology. It is canonical doctrine. Yet it is almost never invoked by the Philippine Church when addressing oligarchic extraction. The Church critiques specific acts of corruption — Marcos-era theft, Arroyo scandals, Duterte extrajudicial killings — but stops short of naming the system itself as sinful.

Why? Because the Church's financial model depends on donations from the architects of that system.

Section 03

Social Sin and Structural Harm

The doctrine of social sin was formulated to address exactly the kind of structural harm that defines the Philippine oligarchy. But the Church's application of the doctrine has been selective.

In October 2025, the CBCP Episcopal Commission on Doctrine of the Faith issued guidance on "tainted donations." The guidance stated that the Church should refuse donations from "proceeds of corruption, exploitation, or activities contrary to Gospel values." But the guidance provided no enforcement mechanism, no audit requirement, and no specific definition of what constitutes "exploitation."

This is not an oversight. It is a design feature. The guidance allows the Church to maintain the appearance of moral clarity while preserving its donor relationships.

The Church can simultaneously condemn oligarchic greed in sermons and accept oligarchic donations in practice, because the doctrine of social sin is invoked only when politically convenient and never when it threatens institutional funding.

Consider the taxonomy of harm. The Independent Research Consultancy's 2026 audit identified six categories of oligarchic extraction:

  • Marcos-era wealth extraction: $165–230B (6–9× total foreign grant ODA 1986–2025)
  • Telecoms market manipulation: ~$152B (342 years of ODA to offset)
  • Power sector rent extraction: ~$8B/year (21% of all OFW remittances annually)
  • Land banking (speculative hoarding): $53–91B cumulative (118–203 years of ODA)
  • SEC fraud (Villar case): $14–20B (9–12 years of grant ODA)
  • Offshore untaxed elite yield: ~$4.5B/year (2.8× annual ODA)

Every one of these categories fits the canonical definition of social sin. Every one involves structures that systematically transfer wealth from the poor to the rich. Every one is enabled by regulatory capture, political protection, and the failure of institutions — including the Church — to name the harm.

The Church does not apply the doctrine of social sin to these structures because doing so would require severing financial and political ties with the families responsible. Instead, the Church maintains what can only be described as theological doublethink: oligarchic greed is condemned in the abstract, but oligarchic donors are welcomed in the particular.

Section 04

The Church's Financial Relationship with Oligarchs

The financial entanglement between the Philippine Catholic Church and the oligarchic class is not speculative. It is documented in church records, corporate filings, and investigative journalism.

Examples:

  • Jaime Zobel de Ayala (Ayala Corporation, net worth $1.1B): donated $15 million to renovate San Agustin Church in Intramuros. The donation represented approximately 1.4% of his net worth at the time. The church bears plaques commemorating the family's generosity.
  • Manuel Villar (net worth $11.4B, contested): funded construction and renovation projects at Manila Cathedral. The exact amounts are not publicly disclosed, but local reporting places contributions in the tens of millions of pesos.
  • Lucio Tan (net worth $2.3B): donated to various diocesan projects, particularly in his home region of Fujian-ancestry Catholic communities in Manila.

These are not one-off acts of charity. They are recurring patterns. The donations are publicly celebrated. Bishops attend ribbon-cutting ceremonies. Plaques are installed. The oligarch's name becomes part of the church's physical infrastructure.

The arithmetic matters. A $15 million donation from a billionaire represents 0.003% of net worth if we use the lower Forbes estimate. For context, if an OFW earning $17,500/year gave the same percentage of income, she would donate $52.50. But the OFW's $52.50 does not buy a plaque on a cathedral wall or a photo op with a cardinal.

The oligarch's donation buys something more valuable: institutional legitimation. When the Church accepts a donation from an oligarch, it signals that the oligarch's wealth is acceptable — not merely legal, but morally clean enough to fund God's work. This is not a neutral transaction. It is a form of laundering, not of money, but of reputation.

The Church's investment portfolios

The Church's financial relationship with oligarchs extends beyond donations. Dioceses and religious orders hold investment portfolios. While the Church does not publish comprehensive financial disclosures, investigative journalism and leaked documents reveal that Church portfolios include shares in Ayala Land, SM Investments, and other oligarch-controlled conglomerates.

This creates a feedback loop. The Church accepts donations from oligarchs. The Church invests in oligarch-controlled companies. Those investments generate returns that fund Church operations. The Church's financial health becomes structurally dependent on the continued profitability of the oligarchic system.

This is not corruption in the traditional sense. It is alignment of incentives. The Church's institutional interest in preserving its financial base aligns with the oligarchs' interest in preserving their political and economic dominance. The result is a tacit alliance that operates without conspiracy or explicit coordination. The Church does not need to be told not to challenge the system. The system is its benefactor.

The Church's silence on oligarchic extraction is not a failure of moral courage. It is a rational response to financial dependency.

Section 05

Non-Catholic Extraction: Iglesia ni Cristo

Iglesia ni Cristo (INC) operates under a different theological model but serves the same structural function: extracting resources from the working class and converting them into political currency that is traded with the same oligarchic families the Catholic Church serves.

INC membership is estimated at 2–3 million, concentrated in Metro Manila and Central Luzon. The Church's defining political feature is bloc voting: members are instructed to vote as a unified bloc for endorsed candidates. Compliance is enforced through a combination of theological authority (members believe salvation depends on obedience to Church leadership) and social pressure (non-compliance risks expulsion and ostracism).

INC's electoral endorsements are not ideological. They are transactional. The Church endorses candidates in exchange for political access, regulatory favours, and tax exemptions. In 2016, INC endorsed Rodrigo Duterte for president and allied candidates for Senate. In 2022, INC endorsed Bongbong Marcos. The pattern is consistent: INC backs whoever offers the most credible promise of protecting the Church's institutional interests.

The financial model is equally extractive. INC requires members to tithe 10–15% of gross income. For a working-class family earning ₱25,000/month ($450), this is ₱2,500–3,750/month. Over a year, that is ₱30,000–45,000 ($540–810). For context, this is more than many families spend on healthcare or education.

Where does this money go? INC does not publish audited financial statements. The Church's assets are held by a corporate structure that shields financial details from public scrutiny. What is visible: INC owns prime real estate in Metro Manila, operates a television network, and maintains a global network of chapels funded by member contributions.

The political economy is clear. INC extracts resources from its members through tithes. It converts those resources into institutional capital. It trades its voting bloc for political protection. The politicians it endorses are the same politicians who protect oligarchic interests. The system is circular: the working class funds the Church, the Church supports the political class, the political class protects the oligarchs, and the oligarchs extract from the working class.

The commodification of the vote

INC's bloc voting transforms the member's vote from a civic right into a commodity controlled by Church leadership. The member does not choose a candidate based on platform, ideology, or personal judgment. The member votes as instructed, under threat of spiritual and social sanction.

This is not merely "voter mobilization." It is the commodification of democratic participation. The vote is extracted from the member, aggregated by the Church, and sold to the highest bidder — not for cash, but for political favours that protect the Church's tax exemptions, regulatory privileges, and institutional autonomy.

The structure is oligarchic in both form and function. INC leadership operates as a cartel that controls a scarce political resource (a reliable voting bloc) and auctions it to political buyers. The resource is produced by the unpaid labour of members who tithe, volunteer, and comply. The profits accrue to the institution, not the members.

INC does not liberate its members from oligarchic exploitation. It integrates them into it as a rentier class — except the rent is paid by the members themselves.

Section 06

Charismatic Extraction: Quiboloy and the Kingdom of Jesus Christ

Apollo Quiboloy, leader of the Kingdom of Jesus Christ (KJC), represents the most predatory variant of religious extraction in the Philippines. Unlike the Catholic Church's institutional legitimacy or INC's bloc voting, Quiboloy's model is based on charismatic authority combined with explicit criminality.

Quiboloy claims to be the "Appointed Son of God." His followers, estimated at 4–6 million globally (though independent verification is impossible), are required to donate tithes, volunteer labour, and recruit new members. Former members report coercion, psychological manipulation, and forced labour in Church-owned businesses.

In November 2021, Quiboloy was indicted in the US District Court for the Central District of California on charges including sex trafficking, marriage fraud, and bulk cash smuggling. The indictment alleges that Quiboloy and his associates coerced young women into sex under threat of "eternal damnation." The same indictment details a scheme in which members were required to solicit donations under false pretenses, with the funds diverted to Quiboloy's personal accounts.

Quiboloy is not an outlier. He is the logical endpoint of a system in which religious authority is monetized, political protection is purchased, and theological claims are weaponized to extract compliance.

What makes Quiboloy structurally significant is not the criminality itself but the political protection he enjoyed. Quiboloy was a close ally of Rodrigo Duterte. He owned the private jet Duterte used during the 2016 presidential campaign. He was a frequent guest at Malacañang. When the FBI placed Quiboloy on its Most Wanted list in 2021, Duterte publicly defended him, calling the charges "persecution."

This is the pattern. Religious leaders who deliver votes, mobilize followers, and fund political campaigns receive protection from prosecution, regulatory scrutiny, and public accountability. The protection extends to tax exemptions (KJC operates as a tax-exempt religious corporation despite running for-profit businesses), media access (Quiboloy owns Sonshine Media Network International), and immunity from investigation by Philippine authorities.

The protection collapsed only after Duterte left office. In September 2024, Quiboloy was arrested after a month-long standoff at his Davao compound. The arrest was not the result of new evidence. It was the result of a change in political alignment. The new administration — Bongbong Marcos — had no use for Quiboloy and no incentive to shield him.

The extraction model

Quiboloy's financial model is simple: extract resources from members, convert resources into political capital, trade political capital for protection, use protection to expand operations and extract more resources. The model scales because the cost of entry is zero (salvation theology is free to produce) and the marginal cost of extraction is near-zero (members self-police and recruit).

Former members report being required to work without pay in KJC-owned businesses, including farms, media companies, and logistics operations. The work was framed as "service to God." Non-compliance was framed as spiritual disobedience. The wages that would normally be paid were captured by the Church and funnelled into Quiboloy's network of businesses and political operations.

This is not metaphorical extraction. It is literal wage theft, sanctified by theology and protected by political alliance.

The arithmetic is clarifying. Power oligarchs extract five times more annually than the Philippines receives in foreign grants. The Marcos family's estimated harm equals 3.7 years of total OFW remittances. Villar's alleged $14–20 billion SEC fraud equals nine to twelve years of grant ODA. The average OFW sends home approximately $17,500 per year; her family pays $400–800 per year above ASEAN benchmark electricity rates to the same oligarchic class her remittances prop up.

The fiesta drain

The fiesta system operates at a scale that is individually small but collectively massive. If 20 million low-income households spend an average of ₱15,000 ($270) per fiesta per year, the aggregate annual drain is ₱300 billion ($5.4 billion). This is not a precise figure — fiesta expenditures vary widely by region and socioeconomic class — but the order of magnitude is correct.

For comparison, $5.4 billion is 67% of annual electricity rent extraction and 3.4 times annual grant ODA. The fiesta system is a regressive tax on the poor, framed as religious devotion, that redistributes resources upward through retail commerce (food, alcohol, decorations) controlled by the same oligarchic families.

The theology that makes this feel like a moral obligation is the same theology that makes accepting oligarchic extraction feel like an unchangeable fact of life.

Section 08

The Three-Way Extraction System

The evidence across all preceding sections converges on a single structural argument. The Filipino poor are being worked from both ends of the religious system simultaneously — and from within.

From above: Catholic oligarchs donate to the Church from the top down, purchasing institutional legitimacy at 0.003% of their net worth, while the Church's investment portfolios in their companies generate returns that further entrench the relationship.

From below: The INC extracts from its members through tithes and the commodification of their voting franchise, then converts what it collects into political currency traded with the same oligarchs the Catholic Church nominally criticises.

From within: The theology of bahala na, instilled over five centuries, re-codes the experience of exploitation as divine will, suppressing the cognitive dissonance that would otherwise drive collective action.

The fiesta system completes the circuit. Families borrow money to honour their patron saint — a financial drain at retail scale across every barangay — while paying above-ASEAN electricity rates to oligarchic power monopolies and sending daughters to the Gulf to cover the gap. The Church collects from all of them. The oligarchs extract from all of them. The theological infrastructure that makes this feel like the natural order of the universe was installed by the same colonial project that gave the Zobel de Ayala family their original land grants.

The correct investigative framing is not that oligarchs are hypocrites. It is that the Philippine system was specifically designed to make the coexistence of intense devotion and extreme exploitation not merely possible but invisible — to those practising it and to those subjected to it.

The colonial installation of ritual without doctrine, the absorption of animist fatalism into Catholic theology, the Church's financial dependence on oligarchic donations, and the INC's conversion of working-class faith into electoral currency are not aberrations. They are the system.
